Selling to organisations instead of individuals? Then there is no single customer. There is a buying centre: a web of roles, each with its own pains, and you must map and message every one. Watch BasiGo sell an electric bus in Nairobi. The SACCO's directors are the decision makers, and their pain is risk: what if this new technology fails? The owner-investors behind each bus are the buyers, and they think in one number: cost per kilometre. The drivers and route managers are the users, and they care about range, reliability and charging time. Regulators and licensing authorities are the gatekeepers who can quietly stop everything. So look at how BasiGo's offer answers each persona at once. The bus is sold without its battery, cutting the upfront price to around thirty-five thousand dollars, close to a diesel bus. The battery, the scariest and most expensive component, is leased at about sixteen cents per kilometre, a fee that covers charging and maintenance too. Technology risk becomes a predictable operating cost, expressed in exactly the unit the buyer already thinks in. The result: more than one hundred and thirty buses on the road, twenty thousand passengers a day, and around five hundred paid deposits waiting. One product, five personas, one message each.
