Rival number one: indirect competition. It comes in two flavours. A company with a similar product in a different market that expands into yours. And a company serving your market with a different product that later builds one like yours. For an e-moto startup, the dangerous rival may not be another e-moto startup at all. It might be the petrol-bike financier who adds an electric model to its loan book, or the fuel-station chain that bolts battery swapping onto its forecourts. In most cases, it is the indirect competitor that eventually runs you out of business. Rival number two: the status quo. The kerosene lamp. The charcoal sack. The diesel generator. The hand hoe. Familiar, already paid for, good enough. Module three told you to price against it; this module tells you to put it on your map as a competitor with a name and a cost. And rival number three: the industry shift. A technology leap, a currency crisis, a pandemic, a regulatory decision. Ask KOKO Networks. One and a half million households served, seven hundred employees, and the business collapsed within weeks of a single denied authorisation on carbon-credit exports. The force that ends your business may not be a company at all. Put the shifts on your radar.
