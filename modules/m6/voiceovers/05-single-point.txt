Before you commit to any model, run the single-point-of-failure test. Ask: if my most fragile revenue stream disappeared tomorrow, does the business survive? KOKO Networks is the cautionary tale of the decade. Stoves sold at a ninety percent subsidy. Fuel sold below cost. And the entire gap bridged by carbon-credit revenue, roughly six million credits a year. As one board member put it afterwards: carbon revenue was not an add-on; it WAS the business. When Kenya's regulator declined the export authorisation, there was no fallback. One and a half million households, seven hundred jobs, gone in weeks. Now contrast Sanergy's architecture in Nairobi. The layer that is genuinely hard to monetise, community sanitation, is deliberately structured as a subsidised non-profit. The layer that can stand on its own feet commercially, turning waste into fertiliser and insect protein, is built as a for-profit business. Same mission, two engines, and no single regulator, buyer or subsidy can switch the whole thing off. Architect your model the same way: diversify the streams, or split the structure.
