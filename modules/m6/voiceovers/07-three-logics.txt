And when you rethink the model itself, three circular revenue logics recur. One: the long-life model. Sell durable quality, and monetise the long life through parts, maintenance and upgrades. Two: the access model. Keep ownership, and rent the use: Hello Tractor's machines, BasiGo's batteries inside the operators' buses, every swap network's cells. Three: the performance model, the boldest of all. Sell the outcome itself: a crate-day of cooling, a kilometre driven, a swap completed. Look at what this does to incentives. When you sell the outcome, you profit when the product lasts longer, breaks less and costs less to run. For the first time, your engineers and the planet want exactly the same thing. Two practical warnings. Performance models are capital-hungry, because the assets stay on your balance sheet. And they depend on two enabling technologies that Africa happens to have in abundance: remote monitoring through connected devices, and mobile money for tiny, frequent, enforceable payments. Those two rails are why a Nairobi startup can run a performance model that would have been impossible anywhere on Earth twenty years ago.
