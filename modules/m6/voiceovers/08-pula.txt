Let me close the tour with a masterpiece of model design built around one question: who pays? Rose Goslinga and Thomas Njeru founded Pula in twenty fifteen to crack a problem that had defeated the global insurance industry: ninety-seven percent of African smallholder farmers are uninsured. Why? A ten-dollar premium cannot pay for an agent visit, and verifying a claim by farm inspection costs more than the claim itself. Pula redesigned every block. The product: parametric insurance priced from satellite data and field sampling, so no farm visits are needed. The delivery: Pula never sells to farmers directly. The premium is embedded in transactions that already happen: inside a bag of seed as a replanting guarantee, inside a loan agreement, inside a government subsidy programme. And the capture: Pula earns fees from more than one hundred distribution partners, while established insurers carry the underwriting risk, keeping Pula asset-light. The results speak: more than fifteen million farmers insured across twenty-two countries, around forty million dollars paid out, and insured farmers investing more and harvesting more. The lesson generalises far beyond insurance. When your end user cannot pay, do not abandon the market. Find the party who captures value when the problem is solved, the lender, the state, the corporate, the carbon buyer, and charge them.
