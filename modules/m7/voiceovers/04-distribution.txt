Now, selling. The rule that surprises every founder: never outsource sales at the beginning. Founder-led, direct sales only. Two reasons. First, early sales conversations are your richest source of learning, and a distributor filters out exactly the feedback you need to reach fit. Second, channel partners rationally deprioritise a new, unproven product with a small commission pool; your launch will die politely in their pipeline. Sell direct until the product sells repeatably. Then, and only then, industrialise distribution, and study how the winners did it, because in markets where formal retail and advertising barely reach your customer, distribution is half the product. Play one: agent networks. M-Kopa sells through roughly thirty thousand commission-based agents; Sun King fields tens of thousands of door-to-door energy officers. Agents convert local trust into sales, and service into retention. Play two: embedded distribution. Pula reaches fifteen million farmers with almost no direct selling, riding transactions that already happen: seed bags, loans, subsidy programmes. Ask yourself: whose existing channel already touches my customer every week? Play three: aggregators. Hello Tractor's booking agents bundle many smallholders' demand into one tractor route. BasiGo sells through the SACCOs that already run Nairobi's routes. Sell through the structures your market already trusts, not around them.
