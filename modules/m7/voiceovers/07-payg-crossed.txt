Let me show you a crossing, the biggest in African climate business. Off-grid solar spent years stuck in early-adopter territory: donors, hobbyists, NGO pilots. The pragmatist household, the shopkeeper, the teacher paid monthly, did not want a green gadget. She wanted light cheaper than kerosene, from a brand her neighbours vouched for, with zero risk. The companies that crossed rebuilt everything around her arithmetic. Barrier one, upfront cost: removed by pay-as-you-go instalments of about fifty cents a day, below her kerosene spend. Barrier two, trust: removed by local agents, by the neighbour who already owned one, and by the fact that if you stop paying, you return the kit and owe nothing more. Barrier three, incompleteness: removed by whole-product kits: lights, phone charging, radio, service and warranty included. Barrier four, payment friction: removed by mobile money. The result is one of Africa's great consumer categories: Sun King serving around fifty million people, d-light touching two hundred million lives, M-Kopa at seven million customers. The pragmatists came when the product spoke pragmatist: cheaper, safer, no risk, and everyone's doing it.
