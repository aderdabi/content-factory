After the beachhead, three expansion routes. Route one: new customer segments, the chasm route, hardest and highest impact. Route two: new offerings to your existing customers, and the master class is M-Kopa. Having built payment rails, agent trust and credit histories selling solar, it sold the same customers smartphones, more than a million of them, then loans, then insurance. The asset was never the panel; it was the relationship. Route three: new countries. Ampersand crossed from Kigali to Nairobi, BasiGo from Kenya to Rwanda, M-Kopa into Nigeria, Ghana and South Africa. But respect the national chasm: payment rails differ, M-Pesa's dominance is Kenyan, not universal; regulations differ; currencies wobble; and trust must be rebuilt border by border. Pick one route at a time. And hear the closing warning. Twiga Foods raised more than a hundred million dollars to digitise fresh-food distribution, scaled trucks, warehouses and headcount aggressively, and then thin margins on perishables crushed the machine: layoffs, retreat, an asset-light shell of its ambition. Compare the patient compounding of M-Kopa, thirteen years to profit but every cohort sound, or Ampersand's station-by-station discipline. Expansion is earned by unit economics, not by funding rounds. Scale what works. Never scale in order to find out what works.
